Making Connections with The 57 Bus - Despite Attacks, an Underground Church for L.G.B.T.Q. Africans Thrives

The congregation in Nairobi, Kenya, has been forced to move to 10 different locations over 10 years, and yet it has survived as a sanctuary in an increasingly hostile environment.

Connections

Challenging Traditional Norms

In Kenya, a church is breaking from deep-rooted religious beliefs to welcome LGBTQ people—something unheard of in much of Africa. It's a bold move in a place where tradition has long dictated who belongs and who doesn't. This church is redefining what faith and community can look like.

In The 57 Bus, we see another kind of challenge to the norm—one tied to gender identity. The book tells the real-life story of a nonbinary teenager in Oakland and the life-altering consequences of a single violent act. The story forces us to confront how strict ideas about gender can lead to misunderstanding, discrimination, and even harm.

Both stories push back against rigid societal norms—one through a religious institution shifting its stance, the other through an individual's deeply personal journey. They ask us to reconsider what acceptance and inclusion really mean.

The Role of Institutions in Marginalization and Transformation

The Kenyan church's decision to welcome LGBTQ members is radical because it directly challenges the role that religious institutions have traditionally played in reinforcing exclusion. Rather than upholding the status quo, this church is showing that faith can evolve to become more inclusive.

In The 57 Bus, we see how other institutions—schools, the legal system, the media—shape the way marginalized people are treated. The justice system, in particular, plays a major role in determining how one act of violence is handled. Is it about punishment, or is there room for rehabilitation?

Both stories highlight how institutions, whether churches or courts, have immense power to either deepen divisions or create spaces for healing.

Empathy and the Possibility of Redemption

At its core, the Kenyan church's decision to embrace LGBTQ people is an act of radical empathy. Instead of following tradition blindly, it chooses compassion, recognizing the humanity of those who have long been pushed aside.

In The 57 Bus, empathy plays a central role in how the story unfolds. We're asked to see the perspectives of both the victim and the perpetrator—to move beyond a simplistic view of good versus bad and instead consider the complexity of human actions.

Both narratives push us to rethink justice and acceptance. They remind us that people can change, institutions can evolve, and healing is possible when we choose to listen and understand rather than judge.

Grassroots Change as a Catalyst for Broader Societal Shifts

The change happening in this Kenyan church isn't being forced from the outside—it's coming from within the community itself. That's what makes it so powerful. When people take small, courageous steps toward inclusion, they set an example for others, creating a ripple effect that can reshape entire societies.

Similarly, The 57 Bus shows how individual actions—whether harmful or redemptive—can spark larger conversations. A single moment on a city bus leads to discussions about gender identity, restorative justice, and how society responds to difference.

Both stories remind us that lasting change doesn't always come from sweeping policies or global movements. Sometimes, it starts with one church, one conversation, or one act of courage.
